This project uses two datasets provided by the UBC research group studying a Minecraft server.
The first dataset, players.csv, contains 196 rows and 7 columns, with one record per player.
It includes variables such as experience (categorical), subscribe (TRUE/FALSE),
played_hours (numeric), gender, and Age.
There are 144 subscribers and 52 non-subscribers, and Age includes two missing values.
played_hours is strongly right-skewed, ranging from 0 to 223.1 hours.
Overall, this dataset describes who the players are and their total engagement level.

(2) Questions

Broad question:
What player characteristics and behaviors are most predictive of subscribing to the game-related newsletter?

Specific predictive question:
Can a players experience level, total played hours, gender, and age predict whether they subscribe to the newsletter?

The exploratory plots show clear differences in engagement across experience groups.
Most players have very low total playtime, but a small number of Pro and Veteran players accumulate extremely high played hours, creating a strong right-skew.
Subscription behaviour appears related to this pattern: players with higher experience levels tend to have longer play histories and are more likely to subscribe.

The age distribution is concentrated between 15 and 25, with only a few older players.
This suggests that the player base is young overall, and age may have limited variation as a predictor.

These patterns indicate that experience level and total played hours are likely to be informative predictors in modelling subscription behaviour.

(4) Methods and Plan To address the predictive question, I plan to use a classification approach.
A logistic regression model will be applied first because the response variable, subscribe, is binary and the model is interpretable.
A k-nearest neighbours (k-NN) classifier will also be considered to capture potential nonlinear relationships between engagement variables and subscription behaviour.

Before modelling, the player-level data will be combined with session-level features using hashedEmail.
Numeric variables will be standardised, and categorical predictors encoded as factors.
The dataset will be split into 70% training and 30% testing, with 5-fold cross-validation used for model tuning.
Potential limitations include class imbalance, right-skewed playtime, and the relatively small sample size.
